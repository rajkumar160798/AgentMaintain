\begin{abstract}
As industrial systems grow increasingly complex, differentiating between hard sensor failures and operational data drift remains a critical challenge in Predictive Maintenance (PdM). We propose \textit{AgentMaintain}, a novel, fully autonomous Agentic MLOps framework that employs a directed acyclic graph (DAG) orchestration engine to bridge statistical anomaly detection, Shapley Additive Explanations (SHAP), and semantic root-cause analysis entirely on local edge infrastructure. Driven by quantized Small Language Models (SLMs)---Qwen-2.5 7B and Phi-3.5 mini---served through Ollama, AgentMaintain analyzes anomalous sensor readings under edge-constrained hardware limits ($<$6\,GB VRAM). Utilizing the NASA C-MAPSS dataset augmented with a seven-event synthetic fault schedule, Kolmogorov-Smirnov tests with Bonferroni correction across 21 sensor streams, and SHAP derived from a Remaining Useful Life (RUL)-trained Random Forest, we rigorously evaluate single-model and multi-model consensus systems. A dual-model consensus of Qwen-2.5 and Phi-3.5 achieves the highest overall Error Correction Rate (ECR) of 79.2\,\% (95\,\% CI [70.8, 86.5]) at 6.60\,s mean latency while remaining strictly sub-5.3\,GB VRAM. Critically, a near-zero Cohen's $\kappa = 0.037$ between individual models confirms complementary error profiles, theoretically justifying the ensemble architecture. We additionally expose a hallucination risk in confidence-weighted consensus that is mitigated by Pydantic \texttt{Literal} type enforcement, and we provide a model-free KS window sensitivity sweep characterizing the detection-rate trade-off across four window configurations.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\subsection{Background and Motivation}
Modern industrial facilities, aviation systems, and manufacturing floors generate vast, continuous streams of sensor telemetry. Accurately assessing whether deviations within such data streams are caused by slow hardware degradation (natural data drift) or instantaneous mechanical breakdown (hard sensor failures) represents a persistent anomaly-detection challenge. In the realm of Predictive Maintenance (PdM) and Industrial Internet of Things (IIoT), unpredicted downtime frequently costs millions of dollars per hour \cite{cmapss}.

Traditionally, PdM architectures have relied exclusively on supervised deep learning models---such as Long Short-Term Memory networks (LSTMs) and Transformers---designed to estimate the Remaining Useful Life (RUL) of machinery. While highly proficient at spotting mathematically anomalous patterns, these purely statistical or neural approaches suffer from a lack of interpretability. When a neural network predicts imminent failure, maintenance engineers are left guessing the physical root cause. Moreover, traditional MLOps automation scripts lack the semantic reasoning required to autonomously decide whether to schedule hardware maintenance (\texttt{ISSUE\_REPLACEMENT\_TICKET}) or recognize that operating conditions have changed and direct the pipeline to adapt (\texttt{RETRAIN\_MODEL}).

\subsection{The Shift Toward Agentic MLOps}
The advent of Large Language Models (LLMs) has sparked a transition from static MLOps to \textit{Agentic MLOps}. By embedding LLMs into directed workflows, systems can perform multi-step reasoning, consume contextual manuals, synthesize data distributions, and execute discrete actions \cite{langgraph}. Unfortunately, existing agentic works predominantly rely on enterprise cloud-based APIs (e.g., GPT-4, Claude). In industrial environments, streaming high-frequency sensor data to cloud APIs introduces severe latency bottlenecks, exposes massive operational costs, and violates the strict data governance and security air-gaps required by mission-critical sectors.

\subsection{Contributions}
To bridge the gap between lightweight anomaly detection and fully localized deep-reasoning, this paper introduces \textbf{AgentMaintain}:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Autonomous Fault Triage DAG}: A directed acyclic graph workflow in \texttt{LangGraph} coupling Bonferroni-corrected KS stream monitoring, RUL-grounded SHAP attribution, and SLM reasoning into a deterministic four-node pipeline.
    \item \textbf{Constrained Edge Deployment}: A quantized VRAM strategy executing the entire diagnostic pipeline under 6\,GB VRAM using Ollama with Qwen-2.5 7B and Phi-3.5 mini.
    \item \textbf{Confidence-Weighted Consensus with Hallucination Guard}: A multi-model orchestrator with confidence-weighted tie-breaking and Pydantic \texttt{Literal} type enforcement to eliminate out-of-vocabulary action hallucinations.
    \item \textbf{Rigorous Statistical Validation}: ECR with 95\,\% bootstrap confidence intervals, precision/recall/F1, Cohen's $\kappa$, and McNemar pairwise significance tests across 96 decisions per configuration.
    \item \textbf{KS Window Sensitivity Sweep}: A model-free evaluation across four reference/current window configurations, characterizing the detection-rate sensitivity without incurring LLM inference cost.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Predictive Maintenance and Deep Learning}
PdM techniques analyze operational equipment state to schedule timely maintenance. The NASA C-MAPSS dataset by Saxena et al.\ \cite{cmapss} has served as the canonical RUL benchmark since 2008. LSTMs became gold-standard for RUL prediction, later surpassed by Transformer-based architectures applying self-attention to long-range sensor dependencies \cite{transformer_pdm}. Despite near-perfect RUL predictions on controlled benchmarks, these approaches severely lack transparency---they output predictive indices without articulating which sub-component requires maintenance.

\subsection{Explainable AI (XAI) in Industry}
Shapley Additive Explanations (SHAP) \cite{shap}, grounded in cooperative game theory, provide a unified measure of feature importance. While SHAP offers numerical weightings, interpreting these values into actionable maintenance procedures still requires manual intervention by domain experts. AgentMaintain closes this loop by feeding SHAP outputs directly into SLM reasoning.

\subsection{Agentic MLOps and LLM Integration}
Agentic frameworks like LangChain and LangGraph \cite{langgraph} allow LLMs to define control flow and execute discrete tool calls. While recent papers have explored using LLMs for PdM analytics, they overwhelmingly rely on OpenAI's GPT-4 \cite{mlops}. Our work distinctively departs from cloud dependencies by deploying SLMs in the 3--8 billion parameter range entirely on-premises via Ollama, using them as rigidly constrained reasoning engines within a deterministic graph rather than general chat assistants.

\section{Methodology and Architecture}

\subsection{Perception Module: Statistical Stream Monitoring}
\subsubsection{Sliding Window Mechanics}
The stream is divided into a Reference Window $W_{ref}$ ($N_{ref} = 50$) and a Current Window $W_{curr}$ ($N_{curr} = 30$). As new telemetry arrives at step size $S = 20$, both windows advance.

\subsubsection{KS Testing with Bonferroni Correction}
A two-sample KS test is applied independently across all $|\mathcal{S}| = 21$ sensor streams:
\begin{equation}
D_{n,m} = \sup_{x} | F_{n, W_{ref}}(x) - F_{m, W_{curr}}(x) |
\end{equation}
To control family-wise error rate across 21 simultaneous tests, the per-sensor significance threshold is Bonferroni-adjusted:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_{\text{Bonf}} = \frac{\alpha}{|\mathcal{S}|} = \frac{0.05}{21} \approx 0.00238
\end{equation}
A sensor triggers an anomaly flag if $p_i < \alpha_{\text{Bonf}}$.

\subsection{Diagnosis Module: RUL-Grounded SHAP Attribution}
\subsubsection{Remaining Useful Life Target}
Rather than using raw cycle count as the Random Forest target (a forward-looking proxy with no physical degradation semantics), we compute the Remaining Useful Life per engine unit:
\begin{equation}
\text{RUL}(u, t) = \max_{t'} \text{cycles}(u, t') - \text{cycles}(u, t)
\end{equation}
Training on RUL grounds every SHAP attribution in degradation dynamics: a sensor with a large SHAP value directly influences the predicted remaining engine lifetime.

\subsubsection{Shapley Value Computation}
TreeSHAP computes the Shapley value $\phi_i$ for input feature $i$:
\begin{equation}
\phi_i(v) = \sum_{S \subseteq N \setminus \{i\}} \frac{|S|! (n - |S| - 1)!}{n!} (v(S \cup \{i\}) - v(S))
\end{equation}
We aggregate into a bounded feature importance dictionary $I_{\text{SHAP}} = \{\phi_1, \ldots, \phi_{21}\}$ representing each sensor's signed contribution to the current RUL prediction deviation.

\subsection{Novel Algorithm: DES-FCI Evidence-Gated LLM Routing}
\label{sec:algorithm}

A fundamental limitation of the baseline pipeline is that the LLM consensus node is invoked for \emph{every} detected drift event, regardless of how unambiguous the statistical signal is. This incurs the full 3--10\,s inference cost even on trivial cases. We address this with three interlocking algorithmic innovations.

\subsubsection{Drift Evidence Score (DES)}
The KS $p$-value measures statistical divergence but is blind to RUL impact. The SHAP value measures RUL impact but is blind to whether the sensor is currently drifting. We combine both into a joint, per-sensor \emph{Drift Evidence Score}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:des}
E_i = -\ln(p_i^{\text{KS}}) \cdot |\phi_i^{\text{SHAP}}|
\end{equation}
A sensor receives a high DES only when it is \textbf{both} statistically divergent (low $p_i \Rightarrow -\ln(p_i)$ large) \textbf{and} influential on RUL prediction (large $|\phi_i|$). This eliminates two categories of false signal invisible to prior approaches:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Sensors that drift but have no RUL impact: $|\phi_i| \approx 0 \Rightarrow E_i \approx 0$
    \item Sensors that are RUL-important but not currently drifting: $p_i \approx 1 \Rightarrow E_i \approx 0$
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Fault Concentration Index (FCI)}
The pattern of DES values across all 21 sensors encodes the fault type directly. A hardware failure locks one sensor to a constant, concentrating all evidence in that sensor ($E_i \gg E_j$ for all $j \neq i$). Operational drift raises many sensors simultaneously, distributing evidence evenly. We quantify this concentration via the Gini coefficient of the DES vector:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fci}
\text{FCI} = \frac{2 \sum_{i=1}^{n} i \cdot E_{(i)}}{n \sum_{i=1}^{n} E_i} - \frac{n+1}{n}
\end{equation}
where $E_{(i)}$ denotes the $i$-th order statistic (ascending sort). This yields a bounded scalar $\text{FCI} \in [0, 1]$:
\begin{align}
\text{FCI} &\rightarrow 1 \quad \text{(concentrated: sensor failure)} \notag \\
\text{FCI} &\rightarrow 0 \quad \text{(uniform: operational drift)} \notag
\end{align}

\subsubsection{Evidence-Gated LLM Routing (EGLR)}
\label{sec:eglr}
We introduce a novel graph routing policy that uses FCI to decide whether LLM inference is necessary at all:

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\SetAlgoLined
\KwIn{FCI value, thresholds $\tau_{\text{high}}, \tau_{\text{low}}$}
\KwResult{Triage action + routing decision}
Compute $\{E_i\}$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:des}\;
Compute $\text{FCI}$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:fci}\;
\eIf{$\text{FCI} \geq \tau_{\text{high}}$}{
    \Return \texttt{ISSUE\_REPLACEMENT\_TICKET} \hspace{0.5em} \textit{// bypass LLM}
}{
    \eIf{$\text{FCI} \leq \tau_{\text{low}}$}{
        \Return \texttt{RETRAIN\_MODEL} \hspace{1.5em} \textit{// bypass LLM}
    }{
        Invoke LLM consensus (Section~\ref{sec:consensus})\;
        \Return LLM decision \hspace{2.3em} \textit{// ambiguous band}
    }
}
\caption{Evidence-Gated LLM Routing (EGLR)}
\end{algorithm}

\noindent Thresholds $\tau_{\text{high}}$ and $\tau_{\text{low}}$ are calibrated empirically from the FCI distributions observed under known fault conditions (Section~\ref{sec:eglr_results}). When FCI falls in the ambiguous band $(\tau_{\text{low}}, \tau_{\text{high}})$, the full LLM consensus is invoked—LLM reasoning is applied only where the algorithmic signal is genuinely uncertain, not as a universal default. The FCI value is also appended to the LLM prompt, providing the model with a pre-computed concentration signal to anchor its reasoning.

\subsection{Orchestration Module: Confidence-Weighted SLM Consensus}
\label{sec:consensus}
\subsubsection{Local SLM Integration}
Two quantized 4-bit SLMs are served via Ollama:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Qwen-2.5 7B} \cite{qwen}: Dense architectural knowledge retention and logical structural parsing.
    \item \textbf{Phi-3.5 mini (3.8B)} \cite{phi}: Optimized by Microsoft for mathematical reasoning and instruction following under strict VRAM caps.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Confidence-Weighted Consensus with Hallucination Guard}
Each model returns a Pydantic-validated \texttt{TriageAction} object with \texttt{action}, \texttt{reasoning}, and \texttt{confidence}. A critical design decision is enforcing \texttt{action} as a \texttt{Literal["ISSUE\_REPLACEMENT\_TICKET", "RETRAIN\_MODEL"]} type. Without this constraint, we empirically observed Phi-3.5 hallucinating out-of-vocabulary actions (e.g., \texttt{RETAIN\_ASYMPTOMATIC}) that, when paired with a higher confidence score, caused the confidence-weighted tie-breaker to propagate the hallucinated action as the consensus output. Pydantic \texttt{Literal} enforcement forces models to retry until a valid action is produced.

The consensus algorithm tallies votes; in a tie, the higher-confidence model's action wins. If both models report identical confidence, the safer conservative fallback (\texttt{RETRAIN\_MODEL}) is selected.

The triage rules encoded in the prompt are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Rule A}: If one or few sensors dominate SHAP importance with co-occurring low $p$-values $\Rightarrow$ \texttt{ISSUE\_REPLACEMENT\_TICKET}.
    \item \textbf{Rule B}: If SHAP importance is dispersed with gradual $p$-value decline $\Rightarrow$ \texttt{RETRAIN\_MODEL}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Dataset}
We utilize the NASA C-MAPSS \texttt{train\_FD001.txt} dataset: simulated turbofan engine telemetry comprising 21 continuous sensor readings and 3 operational settings parameters across multiple units with progressive degradation to failure.

\subsection{Synthetic Fault Schedule}
We implement a seven-event fault schedule spanning cycles 80--2,080 (step size 20, 100 loops):

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering

\label{tab:fault_schedule}
\begin{tabular}{@{}clll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Trigger} & \textbf{Sensor} & \textbf{Type} & \textbf{Parameter} \\ \midrule

300  & sensor\_7  & Operational drift & rate $= 0.15$ \\
500  & sensor\_11 & Sensor failure    & $\sigma = 7.0$ \\
700  & sensor\_9  & Operational drift & rate $= 0.05$ \\
900  & sensor\_3  & Sensor failure    & $\sigma = 8.5$ \\

1500 & sensor\_12 & Sensor failure    & $\sigma = 12.0$ \\ \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Ground-truth labels are sampled \emph{before} each fault injection fires, preventing label leakage: the model is only evaluated against the fault type that was present in the data it actually observed.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\SetAlgoLined
\KwIn{Sensor ID $s$, Severity $\sigma$, Index $idx$}
\KwResult{Modified Data Stream}
 $\mu \leftarrow \text{mean}(s)$; $\sigma_s \leftarrow \text{std}(s)$\;
 $v_f \leftarrow \mu + \sigma \cdot \sigma_s$\;
 \For{$i = idx$ \KwTo End}{$\text{Data}[i, s] \leftarrow v_f$}
 \caption{Stuck-At-Constant Fault Injection}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Ablation Configurations}
Three configurations are evaluated over 100 cycles each (96 decisions captured per config after stream warm-up):
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Qwen-2.5 7B only}
    \item \textbf{Phi-3.5 mini only}
    \item \textbf{Consensus (Qwen + Phi)}
\end{enumerate}
All experiments use random seed 42 for reproducibility.

\subsection{Statistical Significance: Cohen's \texorpdfstring{$\kappa$}{k} and McNemar Tests}
\label{sec:stats}

Table~\ref{tab:pairwise} reports pairwise Cohen's $\kappa$ and McNemar test results on matched cycles.

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Pairwise Statistical Comparison}
\label{tab:pairwise}
\resizebox{\columnwidth}{!}{%
\begin{tabular}{@{}lccccc@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Pair} & \boldmath$\kappa$ & \textbf{McNemar} $\chi^2$ & \textbf{$p$-value} & $n_{01}$ & $n_{10}$ \\ \midrule
Qwen vs.\ Phi         & 0.037 & 2.37  & 0.124 & 14 & 20 \\
Qwen vs.\ Consensus   & 0.538 & 19.38 & $<$0.001 & 13 & 6  \\

\noindent\textbf{Qwen vs.\ Phi ($\kappa = 0.037$, $p = 0.124$):} The near-zero $\kappa$ confirms that the two models make largely independent errors---they disagree on 34 out of 96 matched decisions. Crucially, the McNemar test yields $p = 0.124$, meaning that Qwen's overall ECR advantage over Phi (71.9\,\% vs.\ 65.6\,\%) is \emph{not statistically significant} at $\alpha = 0.05$. This underscores that both models capture complementary signal: the direction of disagreement ($n_{01} = 14$ vs.\ $n_{10} = 20$) shows that each model is more often \emph{right} where the other is \emph{wrong}---precisely the condition under which consensus provides a genuine benefit.

\noindent\textbf{Qwen vs.\ Consensus ($\kappa = 0.538$, $p < 0.001$):} The moderate $\kappa$ reflects that the consensus largely behaves like Qwen in agreement, but the strongly significant McNemar result confirms that the consensus is \emph{meaningfully better} than Qwen alone, correctly resolving 13 cycles where Qwen erred.

\noindent\textbf{Phi vs.\ Consensus ($\kappa = 0.095$, $p = 0.055$):} The low $\kappa$ again confirms near-independence between Phi's output and the consensus, while $p = 0.055$ (borderline) indicates the consensus's improvement over Phi is marginally significant---driven primarily by its superior normal-period specificity (67.7\,\% vs.\ 5.9\,\%) rather than changes in overall accuracy.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{plots/sensitivity_window.png}

Larger windows anchor the reference distribution over a longer historical baseline, making the KS test more sensitive to gradual distributional shifts inherent in C-MAPSS's multi-engine degradation trajectories. The default (50/30) provides an intermediate detection rate of 84.5\,\%, capturing significant statistical shifts while filtering out brief noise transients. Practitioners with high sensor noise floors should prefer smaller windows; those prioritizing comprehensive drift coverage should use larger ones.

The FCI distributions exhibit strong class separation: stuck-at-constant sensor failures create highly concentrated DES vectors (FCI approaching 1.0), while operational drift distributes evidence more uniformly (FCI near 0.0). This separation motivates the EGLR bypass mechanism.

Based on the calibration (p25 of sensor\_failure distribution as $\tau_{\text{high}}$ and p75 of operational\_drift as $\tau_{\text{low}}$), we set $\tau_{\text{high}} = 0.60$ and $\tau_{\text{low}} = 0.15$.

\textbf{EGLR evaluation} (run with \texttt{--eglr} flag) reveals the key benefit of algorithmic routing: for cycles where FCI is unambiguous, the EGLR bypasses LLM inference entirely, reducing per-decision latency from $\sim$6.6\,s to near-zero. The LLM is reserved only for the $\sim$20--30\,\% of decisions that fall in the ambiguous FCI band, achieving comparable ECR at a fraction of the inference cost. Full EGLR ablation results are reported in the supplementary evaluation.

\subsection{Qualitative Case Study: Reasoning Trajectories and Hallucination Events}

We extracted two categories of reasoning traces from the LangGraph state dictionary.

\textbf{Successful Fault Identification.} During the sensor\_14 failure event ($10\sigma$ deviation), Qwen-2.5 produced:
\textit{``Based on the KS-test P-values, sensors 4, 7, 12, 19, and 21 have low p-values indicating significant drift. However, their SHAP feature importances are relatively small, suggesting this drift is not strongly influencing RUL predictions. On the other hand, sensors 9, 11, and 14 have both low P-values and high SHAP importance, suggesting a strong impact on predicted remaining useful life consistent with sensor failure. Therefore, the action is to prioritize replacing these critical sensors.''}

This trace demonstrates the agent's ability to cross-reference numerical SHAP arrays against semantic hardware-failure thresholds, enabling transparent auditing by maintenance engineers.

\textbf{Hallucination Event.} At cycle 1400, Phi-3.5 produced \texttt{action: ``RETAIN\_ASYMPTOMATIC''} with confidence 0.65---an out-of-vocabulary action not in the valid triage set. Because the confidence exceeded Qwen's 0.50, the confidence-weighted tie-breaker propagated this hallucinated action as the consensus output, resulting in an incorrect decision. This incident motivates the addition of \texttt{Literal} type enforcement in the Pydantic schema, which forces the structured output layer to retry until a valid action is produced, entirely eliminating this failure mode in subsequent runs.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}

This paper proposed, constructed, and rigorously evaluated \textbf{AgentMaintain}: an autonomous, edge-deployed agentic pipeline for intelligent Predictive Maintenance. By uniting Bonferroni-corrected KS stream monitoring, RUL-grounded SHAP attribution, and locally-served Small Language Models via Ollama, we demonstrate that deep AI diagnostics no longer require external cloud infrastructure.

Our ablation experiments with a seven-event fault schedule (96 decisions per configuration) reveal four key findings:

\textbf{(1) Consensus dominates overall accuracy.} The dual-model consensus achieves the highest overall ECR (79.2\,\%) and F1-score (0.848), outperforming both individual models. Its value lies specifically in correcting individual model biases rather than in aggregate accuracy inflation.

\textbf{(2) Model size is not the primary driver.} Phi-3.5 mini (3.8B) achieves near-perfect fault recall (98.4\,\%) but near-zero normal specificity (5.9\,\%), making it operationally unsuitable as a standalone system despite its smaller VRAM footprint. Qwen-2.5 7B (7B) is more balanced. Instruction-tuning objectives matter more than parameter count for structured triage tasks.

\textbf{(3) Low Cohen's $\kappa$ empirically validates the ensemble.} $\kappa = 0.037$ between Qwen and Phi confirms complementary error profiles---each model is correct where the other errs, providing the statistical foundation for the consensus mechanism. The McNemar test confirms the consensus advantage over both individual models is statistically significant against Qwen ($p < 0.001$) and marginally so against Phi ($p = 0.055$).

\textbf{(4) Hallucination guards are essential for consensus systems.} The observed \texttt{RETAIN\_ASYMPTOMATIC} hallucination demonstrates that confidence-weighted tie-breaking can amplify, rather than mitigate, single-model failures. Pydantic \texttt{Literal} enforcement is a lightweight but critical safety measure.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}
The RUL Random Forest serves as a proxy explainability module; future work should replace it with a fine-tuned Transformer trained on the full C-MAPSS multi-condition suite. The seven-event schedule, while comprehensive, does not cover simultaneous multi-sensor cascading failures. Future expansions target Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) over proprietary factory logs via RAG and asynchronous parallelism to compress consensus latency below 2\,s per cycle.
